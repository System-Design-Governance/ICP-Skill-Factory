## Description

This skill enables the project manager to track and manage the technical scope of the contract throughout the project lifecycle. It monitors scope baseline versus actual delivery, identifies scope creep, and manages scope change through formal change control processes to ensure alignment between contractual obligations and engineering deliverables.

## Standards

IEC 62443-4-1 (SD-2: Systems Security Requirements and Impact Analysis), IEC 62443-3-3 (Verification and Validation), and organizational contract management and project governance standards.

## IEC 62443 Lifecycle Stages

R1 (Requirements), R2 (Design), R3 (Implementation), R4 (Verification)

## Acceptance Criteria

### Scope Baseline Definition
- Contract technical scope is documented with clear delineation of included deliverables, excluded items, and assumptions.
- Scope baseline is approved by customer/stakeholder and project management and documented in the project plan.
- Scope is mapped to work breakdown structure (WBS) and product breakdown structure (PBS) with traceability.
- Success criteria for scope completion are defined and measurable.

### Scope Monitoring & Reporting
- Actual delivery progress is compared against scope baseline at defined reporting intervals (weekly, biweekly, monthly).
- Scope variance is calculated and reported with root cause analysis for deviations.
- Scope status is communicated to project management and customer/stakeholder through status reports.
- Cumulative scope changes are tracked and reported separately from baseline to maintain traceability.

### Scope Creep Identification
- Scope creep indicators are monitored (unplanned requests, feature additions, specification changes).
- Potential scope creep is identified and escalated to project management with impact assessment (schedule, budget, risk).
- Customer/stakeholder requests outside contract scope are evaluated and documented.
- Impact of scope creep on security requirements and verification/validation plans is assessed.

### Formal Scope Change Management
- Scope changes are processed through formal change control (SK-D10-002, SK-D10-003) with documented impact analysis.
- Change requests include assessment of impact on contractual obligations, schedule, budget, resources, and risk.
- Approved scope changes are reflected in updated scope baseline, WBS, and project plan.
- Change traceability is maintained across all affected project documentation.

### Scope-to-Deliverables Alignment
- Contractual deliverables are mapped to engineered artifacts (design documents, security analysis records, test reports, etc.).
- Completeness of deliverables against contract scope is verified prior to customer/stakeholder acceptance.
- Gaps between contracted scope and delivered artifacts are identified and resolved through change control.

## Inputs

- Contract statement of work (SOW) with technical scope definition
- Project charter and approved project plan
- Work breakdown structure (WBS) and product breakdown structure (PBS)
- Stakeholder requirements documentation
- Change request log and approved change register
- Risk register with scope-related risks
- Verification/validation plan and test specifications
- Labor hour estimation baseline (SK-D14-006)

## Outputs

- Approved scope baseline with traceability matrix
- Scope status reports with variance analysis
- Scope change requests and impacts
- Updated WBS/PBS reflecting approved scope changes
- Scope completion verification records
- Scope-to-deliverables traceability matrix

## Roles — Competency Requirements

- Understanding of contract management and SOW interpretation
- Familiarity with work breakdown structure (WBS) and product breakdown structure (PBS) development
- Project planning and scheduling skills
- Ability to assess impact of changes on schedule, budget, and risk
- Technical knowledge of ICP architecture and engineering approach
- Communication and negotiation with customer/stakeholder

## Estimated Effort

- Percentage of contract scope completed on schedule
- Scope variance (planned vs. actual) at project milestones
- Number and magnitude of scope changes processed
- Accuracy of scope change impact predictions versus actual outcomes
- Customer/stakeholder satisfaction with scope management
- Time to resolution of scope-related issues

## Description — Integration Notes

Contract technical scope tracking is a primary control point for project success, preventing hidden commitments and misalignment between customer expectations and engineering delivery. Scope changes drive labor hour re-estimation (SK-D14-006) and must be formally controlled (SK-D10-002, SK-D10-003) to maintain project commitments. Scope management must coordinate with risk management to ensure security-related scope is not inadvertently reduced or deferred.
