## Description

Design load management strategies for demand response, load shedding, and load balancing in power distribution networks. This skill covers load priority classification of distribution feeders and consumer groups, development of automatic load shedding schemes with well-defined shed stages, and integration of DERMS-based demand response for demand-side flexibility. Design considers operational constraints (voltage stability, thermal limits, frequency support), economic optimization (minimizing demand charges, maximizing asset utilization), and customer service impact (avoidance of unnecessary shedding, fair distribution of load reduction). The output is a formalized Load Management Strategy and detailed implementation specification that guides control algorithm design in the EMS/DERMS system and informs operator procedures for manual intervention.

## Inputs

- Electrical Network Architecture and topology analysis (from SK-D02-001 ⏳: OT Network Topology Design, specialized for power distribution)
- Electrical system model: feeder capacities, transformer ratings, conductor thermal limits, voltage constraints
- Load profile data: historical consumption patterns, peak demand scenarios, seasonal variations
- Distributed Generation (DG) and energy storage capacity and characteristics
- Customer load category definitions and demand flexibility assessment (which loads can be interrupted, curtailed, or shifted)
- Regulatory requirements for demand response and load shedding (grid codes, utility policies)
- Economic constraints: cost of energy, demand charges, penalties for underfrequency load shedding (UFLS)
- System Architecture Design and EMS/DERMS Functional Specification (from SK-D05-001 ⏳ and SK-D05-002 ⏳)

## Outputs

- Load Management Strategy Document: overall philosophy and governance for load management, including performance objectives (peak reduction targets, response time targets, shedding fairness principles)
- Load Priority Classification and Shedding Scheme Definition: categorized load groups (critical/high/medium/low priority), automatic shed stages with load reduction amounts and activation triggers (frequency thresholds, voltage thresholds, real power margin thresholds)
- Demand Response Integration Specification: interface with DERMS for demand-side flexibility, aggregator enrollment, incentive mechanisms, and telemetry requirements
- Load Balancing Algorithm Specification: rules for distributing flows across feeders and assets to optimize utilization and minimize losses (may leverage power flow optimization or simple rules-based distribution)
- Load Shedding Testing and Validation Plan: procedure for commissioning shedding schemes and validating performance under simulated and real contingency scenarios
- Control Logic Specification: detailed state machines and algorithms for automatic load shedding and demand response dispatch (feeds SK-D05-002 ⏳ control design)

## Tools

- ETAP / PSS/E / OpenDSS (electrical system modeling and load flow analysis)
- MATLAB/Simulink (load management algorithm development, load shedding testing)
- Python / R (demand response data analysis, load forecasting, optimization)
- Excel / structured markdown (load priority classification tables, control specification)
- Optimization solvers (linear programming, convex optimization for demand response dispatch)

## Standards

- IEEE 1015 — IEEE Guide for Induction Machinery Maintenance Testing and Failure Analysis: load characteristics and motor stress considerations for shedding
- IEEE 1366 — IEEE Guide for Electric Power Distribution Reliability Indices: metrics for load shedding impact and recovery
- IEC 60909 — Short-Circuit Currents in Three-Phase AC Systems: analysis relevant to load shedding trigger condition determination
- NERC EOP-005 — Underfrequency Load Shedding: standard procedures and requirements for automatic UFLS schemes (North America)
- IEC 62443-3-3 — System Security Requirements and Security Levels: security considerations for load management functions
- IEEE Std 1547 / IEC 61727 — Distributed Generation (DG) connection standards affecting load-DG interaction

## IEC 62443 Lifecycle Stages

- R2 (Detailed Design — load management strategy and shedding schemes finalized)

## Roles

- SYS (System Architect) — primary: develops overall load management strategy, shed schemes, optimization approach
- Electrical Engineer / Power Systems Specialist — supports with load flow analysis, stability assessment, shedding trigger conditions
- DERMS/EMS Specialist — designs integration with demand response platforms and control algorithms
- Operations Manager — defines operational priorities and fairness principles for load shedding
- STC (Security Technical Consultant) — reviews load management for security implications (denial of service via demand response, authentication of demand signals)

## Dependencies

### Hard Dependencies (Required Before Execution)

- SK-D02-001 ⏳: OT Network Topology Design (power distribution variant) — cannot design load management without understanding network topology, feeder structure, and distribution constraints
- SK-D05-001 ⏳: SCADA/EMS/DERMS Functional and Software Design — cannot design load management strategy without knowing what functions the EMS/DERMS will support
- Electrical system model and network data — load shedding and balancing logic depends on detailed understanding of network capacities and operational limits

### Soft Dependencies (Enhances Effectiveness)

- SK-D05-002 ⏳: SCADA/EMS/DERMS Detailed Software and Control Design — load management algorithms inform control logic design
- SK-D05-014: Frequency Regulation Control Design — UFLS and demand response may be coordinated with AGC and frequency support
- SK-D05-005 ⏳: Customer Load Data Collection and Demand Flexibility Assessment — demand response effectiveness depends on understanding customer flexibility

## Acceptance Criteria

1. Load priority classification clearly defines critical, high, medium, and low priority customer/load groups with documented rationale based on safety, economic, and service criteria
2. Automatic load shedding scheme is complete: all shed stages are defined with specific load amounts and activation triggers (frequency thresholds per NERC EOP-005 or system-specific criteria)
3. Load shedding scheme meets performance objectives: peak reduction targets and response time targets are clearly stated and technically achievable
4. Demand response integration is specified: roles, data exchange requirements, incentive mechanisms, and telemetry paths are clear
5. Load balancing rules or optimization approach is documented with clear algorithms and decision logic
6. Control algorithm specification is detailed enough for implementation in SK-D05-002 ⏳ software design
7. Shedding fairness analysis is documented: load distribution methodology ensures equitable distribution of shedding burden over time
8. Contingency scenarios and test cases defined for commissioning validation
9. Design has been reviewed and approved by SYS, electrical engineer/power systems specialist, operations manager, and STC

## Estimated Effort

| Level | Effort | Notes |
|-------|--------|-------|
| Junior (< 2 yr) | 10–16 person-days | Assumes single-area distribution network with 5–10 major feeders, basic load priority scheme, simple shedding logic; includes load flow studies and documentation |
| Senior (5+ yr) | 5–9 person-days | Same scope; senior can rapidly develop shed schemes and leverage standard UFLS frameworks |

Notes: Complex multi-area or meshed networks with significant DG and demand response may require 1.5–2× effort due to optimization complexity and constraint analysis. Projects requiring demand response platform integration and customer enrollment may add 5–10 person-days.
