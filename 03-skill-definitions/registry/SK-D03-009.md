## Description

Simulate transient stability behavior of power systems under fault and disturbance conditions using time-domain simulation tools (ETAP, PSS/E). Determines critical clearing times, generator stability margins, oscillation damping characteristics, and protection coordination requirements. This skill assesses system resilience to major contingencies (three-phase faults, large load or generator loss) and informs security architecture decisions by identifying equipment that must be protected, control systems that must respond quickly, and protection settings that must be coordinated to prevent cascading failures. Results feed into zone design, critical asset protection strategies, and cybersecurity monitoring of protection relays.

## Inputs

- Power System Model with dynamic generator models (from SK-D03-010: Power System Modeling):
  - Synchronous machine models (subtransient, transient reactances, time constants)
  - Generator control models (automatic voltage regulators, speed governors, stabilizers)
  - Load models (frequency-dependent load behavior, motor startup transients)
  - FACTS device models (if present: SVC, STATCOM, HVDC controllers)
- Short-circuit analysis and system X/R ratios (from SK-D03-002: Short Circuit Analysis)
- Power flow operating points representing critical conditions:
  - Peak load scenarios
  - Low load (high transmission impedance)
  - High renewable penetration (low synchronous inertia)
  - Post-maintenance conditions (reduced generation or transmission)
- Defined contingency events and fault scenarios:
  - Three-phase faults at each critical bus
  - Single and double-line-to-ground faults
  - Large generation loss (loss of largest unit)
  - Large load loss (loss of critical customer)
  - Transmission line outages
- Protection settings and relay coordination data:
  - Relay models (distance, overcurrent, out-of-step)
  - Breaker clearing times
  - Control system response times (AGC, PSS tuning)
- Critical clearing time and stability margin requirements (from zone/security design)

## Outputs

- Transient Stability Study Report including:
  - Rotor angle, frequency, and voltage time-domain profiles for each synchronous generator and critical bus
  - Generator stability margin assessment: swing curves showing stability or instability outcome
  - Critical clearing time (CCT) for each defined fault scenario (time at which fault must be cleared to avoid instability)
  - System frequency deviation and inertial response during and after fault
  - Oscillation frequency and damping ratio assessment (identification of poorly damped electromechanical modes)
  - Post-disturbance voltage recovery profiles
  - Identification of "first swing unstable" and "multi-swing unstable" scenarios
- Stability Assessment Matrix:
  - Contingency × Operating Condition matrix showing pass/fail stability
  - Identification of critical contingencies and vulnerable operating points
- Protection Coordination Recommendations:
  - Required relay clearing times for stability maintenance
  - Coordination of distance relay zones with generator stability margins
  - Load shedding and generation tripping logic if required for stability
  - Out-of-step protection coordination (if rotor angle swing exceeds stability limit)
- Control System Requirements:
  - Minimum response time for load shedding, generation control, or FACTS devices
  - Damping control tuning parameters (Power System Stabilizer gains, if applicable)
  - Voltage support strategy during transients
- Dynamic Stability Sensitivity Analysis:
  - Impact of control parameter changes on critical clearing time
  - Generator inertia and damping requirements
  - Reactive power support requirements at each critical bus

## Tools

- ETAP (Transient Stability Analysis, Dynamic Event Simulation modules)

## Acceptance Criteria

1. Dynamic system model includes all synchronous generators, AVRs, governors, and loads with validated parameters per IEEE standards or field commissioning data
2. Transient stability simulation covers minimum 15 defined contingency scenarios (3–5 faults per critical bus, multiple operating points)
3. Critical clearing times (CCT) determined for all high-severity contingencies; no "first swing unstable" cases under peak load conditions
4. All generator rotor angle swings remain < 90° during faults and return to stable equilibrium within 5–10 seconds post-fault
5. System frequency deviation stays within ±5% (or customer-specified limit) and returns to nominal within time-window for load shedding, if required
6. Protection relay clearing times shown to be achievable and coordinated with stability margins; no protection coordination conflicts
7. Damping of post-fault oscillations assessed: damping ratio > 0.05 (5%) for critical electromechanical modes
8. Sensitivity analysis quantifies impact of generator inertia reduction (high renewable penetration scenario) on clearing times
9. Transient stability results traceable to security control decisions: equipment protection zones, load shedding hierarchy, critical asset prioritization
10. Study reviewed and approved by SYS, PRAC, and validated against historical disturbance records where available

| Level | Effort | Notes |
|-------|--------|-------|
| Junior (< 2 yr) | 12–18 person-days | Assumes medium system (15–20 generators), 10–15 contingency scenarios, standard generator models; includes simulation, analysis, protection coordination review |
| Senior (5+ yr) | 6–10 person-days | Same scope; senior leverages prior contingency lists, standard protection schemes, and faster model validation |

Notes: Large systems (50+ generators, multi-area) may require 1.5–2× effort. Custom generator control models (microgrids with advanced inverters) add 5–10 days. Protection relay coordination study and sensitivity analysis add 3–5 days. System with high renewable penetration and low inertia may require extended study duration and multiple sensitivity runs.
