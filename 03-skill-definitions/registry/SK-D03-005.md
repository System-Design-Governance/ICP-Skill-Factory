## Description

BESS Capacity Planning is the systematic determination of Battery Energy Storage System (BESS) energy and power ratings to meet defined grid services objectives, including design of charge/discharge cycle protocols, state-of-charge (SOC) management strategies, and specification of EMS/DERMS control interfaces. The skill considers energy and power balance requirements, battery chemistry limitations, cycle life economics, and the cybersecurity implications of energy storage communication with supervisory control systems.

## Description — Scope

This skill encompasses the design of BESS capacity (MWh energy, MW power rating), charge/discharge power profile design for identified grid services (frequency regulation, peak shaving, arbitrage, voltage support), SOC management strategy including minimum/maximum operating points and reserve policies, cycle life analysis considering depth-of-discharge and equivalent full cycles, and specification of BESS-EMS/DERMS communication interfaces. The skill does not include detailed battery chemistry modeling or thermal management design—these are provided by battery system suppliers. The skill boundary excludes detailed power dispatch optimization which is addressed in SK-D05-004, and excludes protective device coordination which is addressed in SK-D04-001.

## Acceptance Criteria

- BESS energy capacity (MWh) selected and justified based on minimum 4-hour discharge duration at rated power
- Power capacity (MW) determined from maximum simultaneous charging, discharging, and ancillary service requirements
- Charge/discharge protocol designed for each identified grid service: frequency regulation (5-minute response), peak shaving (sustained discharge duration), arbitrage (daily or weekly cycle timing)
- State-of-charge management strategy documented: minimum operating SOC (typically 10-20%), maximum operating SOC (typically 80-90%), emergency reserve policy
- Cycle life analysis completed: equivalent full cycle count projected over system lifetime (10-15 years) considering depth-of-discharge, calendar aging, and temperature effects
- Economically justified: levelized cost of energy (LCOE) and cost per MW-hour of stored energy calculated and compared to alternative technologies
- EMS/DERMS communication interface specified: data points (SOC, power command, temperature, health metrics), update frequency, security requirements
- Thermal management and cooling requirements specified for intended charge/discharge duty cycles
- Integration with microgrid or islanding scenarios addressed: minimum SOC requirements for continuity

## Standards

BESS capacity planning is foundational to IEC 62443 security in energy systems. The EMS/DERMS communication interfaces that control BESS charge/discharge are critical security surfaces requiring SL-C controls for authentication, command authorization, and encrypted data transmission. The BESS itself represents critical control infrastructure whose operation must be protected from compromise (SL-D resilience objective). SOC management strategies represent safety-critical functions (maintenance of reserves for emergency response) that must be protected from unauthorized modification. The power rating and response time of BESS affect the system's ability to implement protective controls for voltage and frequency stability.

## Description — Knowledge Requirements

- Energy storage technologies: lithium-ion, lead-acid, flow battery characteristics and limitations
- Battery chemistry: voltage/current characteristics, thermal effects, cycle life degradation
- Energy management system (EMS) and Distributed Energy Resource Management System (DERMS) architectures
- Grid services: frequency regulation, voltage support, peak shaving, energy arbitrage definitions and requirements
- Power system frequency response: RoCoF (rate of change of frequency), virtual inertia concepts
- State estimation and SOC prediction algorithms
- Thermal management: battery heating/cooling, ambient temperature effects
- Power electronics: bidirectional converter topology, efficiency curves, losses
- Cost analysis: levelized cost of energy, battery cycle economics
- Communication protocols: Modbus, IEC 60870-5-104, CAN, Ethernet for battery management systems

## Roles

- Proficiency in BESS capacity and duration selection for various grid services and customer applications
- Competency in cycle life modeling and degradation prediction
- Ability to analyze power and energy balance requirements across diverse operating scenarios
- Technical judgment in selecting appropriate SOC operating windows balancing performance and battery longevity
- Integration of BESS with EMS/DERMS control architectures
- Cost-benefit analysis and economic justification of BESS capacity selections
- Communication of BESS capabilities and limitations to grid operators and system planners

## Tools

- BESS performance simulation software: MATLAB Simulink, HOMER Energy, PSCAD for transient response modeling
- Battery lifecycle prediction software: Altair Activate, Simulink Battery Management System modules
- Power balance spreadsheet models: hourly or sub-hourly dispatch simulation
- Cost analysis: LCOE calculation, NPV (net present value) analysis
- Thermal modeling: Newton's law of cooling, battery temperature rise estimation
- Communication protocol specification tools: Modbus register mapping, CAN database specification
- Frequency response simulation: synthetic inertia and virtual synchronous machine control algorithms

## Description — Process Steps

1. Identify grid services and application requirements: frequency regulation, peak shaving, microgrid support, etc.
2. Define power requirement for each service: sustained discharge power, ramp rate, minimum response time
3. Define energy requirement for each service: discharge duration for peak shaving, cycle depth for frequency regulation
4. Determine simultaneous power requirements: sum maximum charging, discharging, and ancillary service power demands
5. Select battery chemistry and technology considering application profile, cost, and maturity
6. Model BESS power and energy capacity: energy = power × duration for each identified service
7. Confirm minimum energy capacity adequate for 4-hour discharge at rated power
8. Define state-of-charge management strategy: minimum operating SOC (reserve), maximum operating SOC (lifespan), emergency reserve
9. Perform cycle life analysis: project equivalent full cycles considering depth-of-discharge, temperature, calendar aging
10. Calculate levelized cost of energy and cost per MW-hour of stored energy
11. Design charge/discharge protocol for each service: power profile, timing, maximum daily cycles
12. Specify thermal management: cooling capacity, ambient temperature range, thermal sensors
13. Design EMS/DERMS communication interface: data point list, update frequency, security requirements
14. Model system response in microgrid islanding scenarios: confirm adequate energy for critical loads
15. Document all assumptions and justify capacity selections

## Description — Decision Logic

BESS capacity planning results guide critical system architecture decisions:
- Energy capacity sizing: minimum 4-hour duration standard vs. extended duration for longer services
- Power capacity rating: determines response time to frequency events and power ramp capability
- SOC operating window: narrower window extends battery life but reduces usable capacity
- Cycle depth strategy: shallow daily cycles (20-30% DoD) extend life but require larger capacity
- Grid service priority: frequency regulation vs. peak shaving vs. energy arbitrage affects charge/discharge scheduling
- Communication latency: EMS command response time must be faster than required grid service response time
- Thermal design: passive vs. active cooling affects operational envelope and capital cost

## Description — Common Errors

- Sizing BESS for peak power without considering energy duration adequacy—high power alone insufficient for grid services
- Neglecting depth-of-discharge effects on cycle life: using aggressive operating window reduces effective cycle count
- Assuming battery manufacturer specifications valid at extreme temperatures without thermal management
- Designing for only normal operating scenarios without considering rapid SOC depletion in contingency conditions
- Overestimating control system response speed without accounting for communication latency in charge/discharge commands
- Treating state-of-charge as measured value rather than estimated value with uncertainty bounds
- Assuming vendor-provided battery management system security is adequate without independent assessment

## Acceptance Criteria — Quality Metrics

- Power capacity justified: maximum of simultaneous charging, discharging, and service requirements documented
- Energy capacity justified: minimum 4-hour duration standard applied, specific energy (Wh/kg) documented
- Cycle life analysis completed: projected cycles over 10-year life span with specified DoD and temperature effects
- Cost analysis: LCOE calculated and compared to alternative technologies for identified services
- SOC management strategy: operating window defined, minimum reserve quantified, emergency policy documented
- EMS/DERMS communication specification complete: protocol choice justified, data latency quantified, bandwidth adequate
- Thermal analysis: maximum temperature during sustained discharge confirmed within battery operating limits

## Outputs

- BESS Capacity Planning report documenting all design decisions, calculations, and justifications
- Capacity specification summary: energy capacity (MWh), power capacity (MW), energy:power ratio, duration at rated power
- Grid services specification: power and energy requirements for each service (frequency regulation, peak shaving, arbitrage, voltage support)
- Charge/discharge protocol design: power profile diagram for each grid service, daily cycle definition, maximum depth-of-discharge
- State-of-charge management strategy: minimum/maximum operating SOC values, emergency reserve quantity, reserve release policy
- Cycle life analysis: projected equivalent full cycles over system lifetime, degradation curve showing capacity fade vs. time
- Economic analysis: levelized cost of energy ($/kWh), cost per MW-hour ($MW⁻¹h⁻¹), payback period for identified services
- Thermal management specification: continuous rated power at ambient temperature, required cooling capacity, temperature sensors
- EMS/DERMS communication interface specification: protocol selection, data point list with units and range, update frequency (seconds)
- System integration diagram: BESS power electronics, DC-AC converter, protection relays, EMS connection points
- Microgrid islanding analysis: minimum SOC for critical load support, estimated duration at specified load
